Cross Analysis of "The Taming Of The Shrew" and "Vinegar Girl"

Module A Rubric Overview: Narratives that Shape our World

Focus:
Students explore a diverse range of narratives from both past and contemporary contexts to understand how stories illuminate and convey ideas, attitudes, and values. The module emphasises the role of storytelling in connecting people across cultures and historical eras, inspiring change or consolidating stability, questioning or affirming cultural practices, sharing collective or individual experiences, and celebrating aesthetic achievement.

Key Areas of Study:

  • Exploration of Narratives:
    Investigate how narratives shape meaning across different modes, media, and forms, and how they influence individual and community identity.
  • Context and Values:
    Analyse how narratives are shaped by the context and values of composers (authors, poets, playwrights, directors, designers) and responders. Consider how narratives can be reimagined or adapted for new audiences.
  • Textual Structures and Language:
    Examine how authors use textual structures, language, and stylistic features (such as setting, voice, point of view, imagery, and characterisation) to craft meaning for specific purposes and audiences.
  • Rhetorical Devices and Conventions:
    Explore how rhetorical devices enhance narrative power, including in persuasive texts. Develop understanding of syntax, spelling, punctuation, and grammar for effect.
  • Creative and Critical Responses:
    Develop skills to evaluate and refine personal use of narrative devices to express complex ideas creatively. Critically evaluate narrative techniques used by other composers.

Skills Developed:
- Critical analysis of how narratives shape and are shaped by culture and context
- Creative composition and experimentation with narrative forms
- Appreciation of the dynamic relationship between storyteller, story, and audience
- Effective use of language and literary techniques for intended effects

This rubric guides students to deepen their understanding of narrative’s role in shaping human experience and encourages both analytical and creative engagement with texts.

Text Overviews and Alignment with Module A Rubric

1. William Shakespeare's The Taming of the Shrew

Overview: The Taming of the Shrew is a comedic play exploring themes of marriage, social expectations, and gender roles in 16th-century Italian society. The central plot revolves around Petruchio's attempts to "tame" the headstrong Katherina into an obedient wife, while a subplot focuses on the efforts to find a husband for her younger sister, Bianca. The play uses wit, farce, and dramatic irony to examine power dynamics and societal norms of the time.

Alignment with Module A:

  • Exploration of Narratives: The play reflects and critiques the societal expectations placed on women, particularly within the context of marriage. It shows how narratives can reinforce or challenge cultural practices.
  • Influence of Context and Values: Shakespeare's play is deeply rooted in the patriarchal values of his era, influencing the portrayal of gender roles. Modern interpretations often critique these values, illustrating how narratives are reinterpreted across time.
  • Narrative Techniques and Conventions: The play utilizes dramatic techniques like dialogue, soliloquies, and comedic timing to explore themes and engage the audience. These conventions create meaning and influence the play's reception.

2. Anne Tyler's Vinegar Girl

Overview: Vinegar Girl is a modern retelling of The Taming of the Shrew, set in contemporary Baltimore. It tells the story of Kate Battista, a spirited but unconventional woman pressured into a marriage arrangement with Pyotr, a Russian scientist, to secure his visa. Tyler's novel explores themes of family obligation, personal identity, and the clash between tradition and modernity.

Alignment with Module A:

  • Exploration of Narratives: As a retelling, Vinegar Girl directly engages with the narrative of The Taming of the Shrew, reimagining its themes in a contemporary context. It explores how narratives are adapted to reflect new values and perspectives.
  • Influence of Context and Values: Tyler's novel reflects modern values regarding gender roles and cultural diversity, offering a critique of the patriarchal assumptions present in Shakespeare's play. The shift in context shapes the narrative and its message.
  • Narrative Techniques and Conventions: Tyler employs a realistic narrative style with detailed character development and social commentary. The use of contemporary language and settings makes the themes accessible to modern readers.

Alignment Between Texts

Both texts align in their exploration of relationships and societal expectations but differ in their approach and values. Shakespeare's play is a product of its time, reflecting patriarchal norms, while Tyler's novel offers a critical reimagining through a modern lens. By comparing the two, students can gain insights into how narratives evolve and adapt to reflect changing social attitudes and values. They examine how the same story can be told in different ways to express different ideas about the world.

TEEA Table: The Taming of the Shrew (William Shakespeare)

Technique Example (Quote) Effect Analysis (in relation to the question)
Soliloquy Katherina: “No shame but mine: I must, forsooth, be forced to give my hand, opposed against my heart.” Reveals her private conflict and emotional turmoil. The soliloquy exposes how Katherina is shaped by the societal narrative of marriage, yet her inner voice suggests a desire to resist and reshape her own story.
Animal Imagery Petruchio: “My falcon now is sharp and passing empty; And till she stoop she must not be full-gorged.” Dehumanises Katherina, likening her to a wild animal to be tamed. This imagery reflects how cultural stories about gender shape perceptions and behaviour, yet the audience is prompted to question and potentially reshape these narratives.
Disguise/Role-play Sly is convinced he is a lord; Lucentio and Tranio swap identities. Undermines the idea of a fixed identity and highlights the performative nature. The use of disguise demonstrates how individuals are shaped by the roles and stories assigned to them, but also how they can subvert or reshape those narratives through performance.
Juxtaposition “I see a woman may be made a fool, If she had not a spirit to resist.” Contrasts submission with resistance. Shakespeare explores how people are shaped by dominant stories, but also possess the agency to resist and reshape them, showing the interplay between story and self.
Rhetorical Speech Katherina: “Such duty as the subject owes the prince, Even such a woman oweth to her husband...” Creates ambiguity about Katherina’s transformation and the sincerity of her speech. The rhetorical structure of her final speech raises questions about whether she has been shaped by the narrative or is strategically reshaping it for her own ends.

The internal monologue shows how Kate’s sense of self is influenced by external stories, yet she questions and negotiates these narratives, embodying the module’s central idea.

Comparison of Two Texts in Relation to Question

Both The Taming of the Shrew and Vinegar Girl vividly exemplify the idea that “stories shape us just as much as we shape them,” as each text reflects and interrogates the social values of its era while also inviting readers to reconsider and reshape those values. Shakespeare’s The Taming of the Shrew is firmly rooted in the patriarchal context of the Renaissance, presenting a world where women’s roles are defined by male authority and societal expectation; Katherina’s resistance and eventual apparent submission both reflect and challenge the dominant narrative of female obedience, illustrating how individuals are shaped by, and can in turn subvert, the stories imposed upon them.

In contrast, Anne Tyler’s Vinegar Girl reimagines this narrative in a contemporary setting, using Kate Battista’s journey to critique and complicate modern gender roles. Tyler’s adaptation preserves the core tensions of the original but allows her protagonist greater agency, showing how stories can be appropriated and reshaped to reflect evolving attitudes towards gender and identity.

Through their respective approaches, both texts demonstrate the enduring power of narrative to construct, reinforce, and ultimately transform personal and collective identities, proving that while we are shaped by the stories we inherit, we also possess the capacity to reshape those stories for new contexts and audiences.
